## Overview
This research program examines a common puzzle: people do not always use their full abilities. I focus on the distinction between **ability** (what a person can do) and **attitude/willingness** (what a person is inclined to do), and how this distinction depends on person–task–situation interactions.

## Key contributions
- **Effectivity and (under/over)achievement:** We argue that narrow achievement measures can misclassify students as under- or over-achieving because students may deploy their abilities in different domains or contexts. This motivates a broader, interaction-focused view of achievement.
- **Attitudinal intelligence:** Empirical work testing components of attitudinal intelligence and how attitudes relate to the deployment of cognitive abilities.
- **Cultural intelligence (CI):** Studies examining the distinction between ability and attitude within CI, including work in a U.S. sample and a cross-cultural project.

## Why it matters
This line of work has practical implications for education and talent development: helping learners and employees not only build skills, but also develop the motivations and attitudes needed to apply those skills effectively in real contexts.
